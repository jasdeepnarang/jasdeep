▲ Business & latest quarter
NVIDIA's fiscal Q1 FY2027 results (reported May 20, 2026) showed record revenue of $81.6 billion, up 85% year-over-year and ahead of the $78.8B Wall Street estimate, with adjusted EPS of $1.87 beating the $1.76 consensus. Data center revenue continues to dominate the mix — now roughly 87-90% of total sales — powered by the Blackwell GPU generation, which CEO Jensen Huang described as sold out across cloud providers. Gross margin sits around 75%. For Q2 FY27, guidance came in at $91.0 billion, above the $86.84B analysts expected. The company also raised its quarterly dividend from $0.01 to $0.25/share and returned roughly $20 billion to shareholders in the quarter via buybacks and dividends combined — a signal of confidence in free cash flow durability even amid heavy capex.
● Why it's in the book
NVIDIA remains the default platform for AI training and increasingly inference — management points to its chips running "every frontier AI model" across hyperscalers, and the new Vera CPU line opens what the company frames as a further $200B addressable opportunity. The entry was sized as a core holding rather than a swing trade: the thesis is multi-year AI infrastructure demand, not a single-quarter print.
■ Risks to watch
Valuation is not cheap at ~30x forward earnings, so even strong beats can produce muted or negative stock reactions if guidance doesn't clear the market's already-high bar (this happened after the May print). Customer concentration among a handful of hyperscalers, and China/export-control policy, remain structural risks outside the company's control.

▲ Business & latest quarter
Broadcom's Q2 FY2026 results (June 3, 2026) showed total revenue of $22.2 billion, up 48% year-over-year, with AI semiconductor revenue of $10.8 billion, up 143% — now roughly half of total revenue. The stock still fell about 3% on the print, not because the AI business disappointed, but because infrastructure software revenue narrowly missed estimates ($7.18B vs $7.32B) and Q3 AI guidance of $16 billion, while up over 200% year-over-year, landed below what the most aggressive buy-side models had priced in. Full-year 2026 AI revenue guidance stands at ~$56 billion (+180%), with management reiterating FY2027 AI revenue above $100 billion.
● Why it's in the book
Broadcom's model is different from NVIDIA's: rather than selling off-the-shelf GPUs, it co-designs custom XPU accelerators directly with hyperscaler customers (Google, Meta, Anthropic, OpenAI among the six core customers cited), each requiring an 18-24 month co-engineering cycle. That creates high switching costs and multi-year revenue visibility once a customer is locked in. Broadcom has also moved into infrastructure partnerships — a deal with Apollo and Blackstone to deploy over 20 gigawatts of AI compute capacity, with a first tranche valued at $35 billion.
■ Risks to watch
This is the largest single position in the book at ~19.8% of NAV, close to a self-imposed 20% single-name guideline — any further appreciation likely triggers a trim discussion rather than letting it run further. The stock is also priced for perfection after a ~40% year-to-date run into the print; the software segment (VMware integration) is a second growth engine that needs to execute alongside the AI story, not just ride on it.

Microsoft's fiscal Q3 2026 results (late April 2026) beat on both lines — adjusted EPS of $4.27 versus $4.06 expected, revenue of $82.89 billion — with Azure guided to 39-40% constant-currency growth for Q4. The company disclosed FY2026 capital expenditure would reach roughly $190 billion, driven partly by soaring memory costs, and gross margin (67.6%) hit its narrowest level since 2022 as data center depreciation scales with the buildout. Microsoft 365 Copilot has crossed 20 million paid commercial seats. Q4 FY2026 results are expected imminently, with analysts (HSBC, as of mid-July) forecasting ~$89.3 billion revenue, +16.8% year-over-year, and Azure growth near 39.6%.
● Why it's in the book
Microsoft is the best-performing position in the book so far, and the thesis was a deep-value one at entry: the shares were bought close to a 52-week low, at a relatively cheap forward multiple for a company with a durable enterprise cloud and productivity franchise plus a real AI monetization path through Copilot and the OpenAI partnership. Diversified revenue (cloud, productivity, gaming, LinkedIn) makes the business less single-threaded than pure-play AI infrastructure names.
■ Risks to watch
Margin compression from the AI infrastructure buildout is real and ongoing — gross margin has been trending down as depreciation scales faster than efficiency gains. GPU supply constraints have, at times, capped how fast Azure can convert demand into recognized revenue. Capex intensity at this scale requires sustained enterprise AI monetization to justify the spend.

Eli Lilly's Q1 2026 results (April 30, 2026) were a large beat: revenue of $19.8 billion, up 56% year-over-year, with adjusted EPS of $8.55 well above the ~$6.66-6.79 consensus. Mounjaro (type 2 diabetes) grew 125% to $8.66 billion; Zepbound (obesity) grew 80% to $4.16 billion in the US alone. Lilly raised full-year 2026 revenue guidance to $82-85 billion (from $80-83 billion) and lifted adjusted EPS guidance to $35.50-37.00. ● Why it's in the book
Lilly holds roughly 60% share of the global GLP-1 market and is positioned as the structural leader in what's likely a decade-long obesity/metabolic disease treatment cycle, now compounded by an oral pill that can expand the addressable patient population well beyond injectables. Beta is low relative to the rest of the book, intended as a partial shock absorber alongside the higher-beta tech/defence sleeves.
■ Risks to watch
This is the largest dollar drawdown in the book, entered close to an all-time high with limited margin of safety — a near-ATH entry into a subsequent broad market pullback explains most of the underperformance so far rather than any company-specific issue. Pricing pressure from US drug-pricing negotiations and lower realized prices (both drugs saw price declines even as volume surged) are an ongoing offset to volume growth. Retatrutide Phase 3 data and continued Foundayo ramp are the next catalysts to watch.

Vertex's Q1 2026 results (May 4, 2026) showed total revenue of $2.99 billion, up 8% year-over-year, with non-GAAP EPS of $4.47. The core cystic fibrosis (CF) franchise remains the volume anchor, with ALYFTREK — its newest CF drug — crossing $1 billion in cumulative revenue since its December 2024 approval. The bigger story is diversification: gene-editing therapy CASGEVY (for sickle cell disease and beta thalassemia) generated $43 million and non-opioid pain drug JOURNAVX generated $29 million in the quarter — together contributing over a quarter of year-over-year growth. Full-year 2026 guidance of $12.95-13.1 billion in revenue (8-9% growth) was reaffirmed. JOURNAVX now has access to over 200 million covered lives, roughly two-thirds of US commercial and government-covered lives, with prescriptions expected to more than triple in 2026 versus 2025.
● Why it's in the book
Vertex holds a near-monopoly in CF treatment with strong pricing power and margins, and is using that cash generation to fund a genuine diversification into new disease areas (sickle cell, acute pain, and a nascent nephrology franchise via povetacicept). The entry was a base-building one — bought mid-range of the 52-week band rather than chasing a breakout.
■ Risks to watch
JOURNAVX's ramp, while directionally strong, remains well below the run-rate needed to hit management's full-year ambition and depends on gross-to-net normalization through H2 2026. CASGEVY's rollout is still small in absolute dollars and gated by the complexity of the gene-therapy treatment/infusion process. The nephrology franchise is early-stage and unproven commercially.

Constellation's Q1 2026 results (mid-May 2026) showed GAAP EPS of $4.49 (versus $0.38 a year earlier) and adjusted operating EPS of $2.74, up 28% year-over-year, beating estimates. Full-year 2026 adjusted operating EPS guidance of $11-12 was reaffirmed. The nuclear fleet — the largest in the US — ran at a 92.3% capacity factor, generating 40 terawatt-hours in the quarter, with roughly 147 TWh of annual capacity available, ~93% of it within the PJM grid footprint that serves much of the US mid-Atlantic. The $26.6 billion Calpine acquisition closed in January 2026, adding gas, geothermal, hydro, wind, and solar assets to reach a combined ~55 gigawatts of generation capacity. The company holds over 5,650 megawatts of long-term clean power agreements with data center and hyperscale customers, including a 20-year power purchase agreement with Microsoft tied to the Crane Clean Energy Center (the former Three Mile Island Unit 1), whose restart is targeted around 2027-2028 pending regulatory sign-off.
● Why it's in the book
Constellation is a way to own the AI buildout's power constraint rather than the compute layer directly — a theme that's structurally uncorrelated with chip-sector sentiment. Firm, carbon-free nuclear capacity is the specific asset hyperscalers are contracting for at premium long-term rates, and Constellation controls more of it than any other US operator.
■ Risks to watch
The stock trades at a rich premium (around 40x earnings) that assumes continued conversion of its available nuclear capacity into new long-term contracts — the market has been watching for confirmation of new hyperscaler deals since a December 2025 FERC order cleared a key regulatory bottleneck for co-located data center service. Calpine integration (2,300 employees, 60+ power plants) is still in its early stages and carries execution risk. Nuclear fleet aging, relicensing, and maintenance costs are longer-duration risks to monitor.
